How to Use This Mortgage Calculator

Select your country (Canada or US), enter your home price, down payment, amortization period, and interest rate. Results update instantly. For Canada, we also calculate your CMHC insurance premium (if applicable) and stress test qualifying payment.

Canada vs US: Key Differences

Canada uses semi-annual compounding by law (Interest Act). This means the effective monthly rate is calculated as:

r = (1 + annual rate / 2)^(1/6) − 1

United States uses monthly compounding, so:

r = annual rate / 12

Both then use the standard amortization formula: M = P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] / [(1+r)ⁿ − 1]

What Is CMHC Insurance? (Canada)

If your down payment is less than 20% in Canada, you are required to purchase mortgage default insurance through CMHC (or Sagen/Canada Guaranty). The premium is added to your mortgage principal:

  • 5%–9.99% down → 4.00% of loan amount
  • 10%–14.99% down → 3.10% of loan amount
  • 15%–19.99% down → 2.80% of loan amount
  • 20%+ down → No CMHC required

What Is the Mortgage Stress Test? (Canada)

Canadian lenders must qualify you at the higher of: your contract rate + 2%, or 5.25%. This stress test payment is shown so you can see what rate you must qualify at — your actual payment will be lower.

Tips for Getting a Lower Mortgage Payment

  • Increase your down payment to 20%+ to avoid CMHC insurance (Canada)
  • Shop lenders — even 0.25% rate difference saves thousands over 25 years
  • A shorter amortization (e.g. 20 years vs 25) saves significant interest
  • Improve your credit score before applying

Understanding Amortization: Why It Matters So Much

Amortization is the length of time it takes to pay off your mortgage in full. In Canada the maximum amortization for insured mortgages (less than 20% down) is 25 years, while uninsured mortgages can go up to 30 years. In the US, 15 and 30 year terms are by far the most common.

The amortization period has a dramatic effect on both your monthly payment and the total interest you pay over the life of the loan. Consider a $600,000 mortgage at 5.5%:

  • 25-year amortization: ~$3,660/month — total interest paid: ~$498,000
  • 20-year amortization: ~$4,100/month — total interest paid: ~$384,000
  • 15-year amortization: ~$4,900/month — total interest paid: ~$282,000

Choosing a 20-year over a 25-year amortization costs about $440 more per month but saves over $114,000 in interest. Use the amortization table in this calculator to see the year-by-year breakdown of your specific situation.

Fixed vs Variable Rate Mortgages

One of the most consequential decisions a borrower makes is whether to choose a fixed or variable interest rate.

Fixed rate mortgages lock your interest rate for the full term (typically 1–5 years in Canada, up to 30 years in the US). Your payment never changes during the term, which makes budgeting predictable. Fixed rates are generally higher than variable rates because the lender is absorbing the risk that rates could rise.

Variable rate mortgages are tied to the lender's prime rate, which moves with the central bank's policy rate (Bank of Canada or US Federal Reserve). When rates fall, your effective rate falls with it — and vice versa. Variable rates have historically been lower than fixed rates over long periods, but they carry the risk of payment increases if rates rise quickly, as many borrowers experienced in 2022–2023.

A common strategy is to choose variable when rates are expected to fall, and lock in a fixed rate when rates are near historical highs. However, predicting rate movements is notoriously difficult even for professional economists. Your risk tolerance and budget flexibility should guide this decision more than any rate forecast.

How Lenders Assess Your Mortgage Application

Mortgage approval is based on several factors beyond just your income. Understanding these helps you prepare a stronger application and potentially qualify for a better rate.

Credit score: In Canada, a score above 680 is generally required for most lenders; 720+ gets you the best rates. In the US, conventional loans typically require 620+, while the best rates go to borrowers with 740+. Check your credit report well in advance and dispute any errors.

Gross Debt Service (GDS) ratio (Canada): This is the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes toward housing costs (mortgage payment, property taxes, heating, and 50% of condo fees). Lenders want this below 32–39% depending on the lender.

Total Debt Service (TDS) ratio (Canada): This includes all debt payments (housing + car loans + credit cards). Most lenders cap this at 44%. In the US, a similar metric called the Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio should ideally stay below 43%.

Employment history: Lenders prefer at least two years of stable employment in the same field. Self-employed borrowers typically need to provide two years of Notice of Assessments (Canada) or tax returns (US) to prove income.

Down payment source: Lenders require proof that your down payment has been in your account for at least 90 days, or that it came from a gifted amount with a signed gift letter. Borrowed down payments are generally not allowed for high-ratio mortgages.

First-Time Home Buyer Programs

Canada — First Home Savings Account (FHSA): Introduced in 2023, the FHSA allows first-time buyers to contribute up to $8,000 per year (lifetime max $40,000) in a tax-free account specifically for purchasing a first home. Contributions are tax-deductible (like an RRSP) and withdrawals for a home purchase are tax-free (like a TFSA). This is one of the most powerful savings vehicles available to Canadian first-time buyers.

Canada — Home Buyers' Plan (HBP): First-time buyers can withdraw up to $35,000 from their RRSP ($70,000 per couple) to use as a down payment. The amount must be repaid to the RRSP over 15 years.

Canada — First-Time Home Buyer Incentive: A shared equity program where the federal government contributes 5–10% toward your purchase in exchange for a proportional share of the home's future value. This reduces your monthly payment without increasing your down payment.

US — FHA Loans: Backed by the Federal Housing Administration, FHA loans allow down payments as low as 3.5% for borrowers with credit scores of 580+. They require mortgage insurance premiums (MIP) but are accessible to buyers who don't qualify for conventional financing.

US — VA Loans: Available to eligible veterans, active-duty service members, and surviving spouses. VA loans require no down payment and no private mortgage insurance, making them one of the most favorable loan types available.

Mortgage Renewal and Refinancing

In Canada, mortgages are not fully amortized over a single term. Instead, you sign a new contract every 1–5 years (the term), at whatever rate the market offers at renewal time. This is a major difference from the US, where a 30-year fixed mortgage locks in your rate for the entire loan duration.

At renewal, you are free to switch lenders without penalty. This is the best time to renegotiate your rate, adjust your amortization, or change from fixed to variable (or vice versa). Start shopping 4–6 months before your renewal date. Even a small rate improvement of 0.25% on a $500,000 mortgage saves approximately $1,250 per year.

Refinancing means breaking your existing mortgage before the term ends to take advantage of lower rates or to access home equity. In Canada this typically triggers a prepayment penalty — usually either 3 months' interest or the Interest Rate Differential (IRD), whichever is greater. The IRD can be very large on fixed-rate mortgages when rates have fallen significantly. Always calculate the break-even point before refinancing.

Making Prepayments to Save on Interest

Most Canadian mortgages allow annual prepayments of 10–20% of the original principal without penalty. Making even modest lump-sum payments dramatically reduces your total interest cost and shortens your amortization.

For example, on a $500,000 mortgage at 5.5% with a 25-year amortization, making a single $10,000 lump-sum prepayment at the end of year one saves approximately $22,000 in interest and cuts 14 months off your amortization. Use the calculator above to model your specific prepayment scenario by reducing the principal field.

Another strategy is to increase your payment frequency to accelerated bi-weekly. Instead of making 12 monthly payments, you make 26 bi-weekly payments — the equivalent of one extra monthly payment per year. This alone can shorten a 25-year mortgage by about 3 years and save tens of thousands in interest with no change to your lifestyle.
